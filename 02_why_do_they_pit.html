Tyres, undercuts, C1 to C5 — F1 strategy looks mad. It's actually one idea, repeated cleverly. Let's walk through it.

It all starts with rubber.

In F1, tyres are the only part of the car touching the road. As they wear, lap times get slower — sometimes by over a second per lap. Fresh tyres = free speed. That's the whole reason they pit.

The rule that makes strategy interesting: in a dry race, every driver must use at least two different tyre compounds. So everyone has to pit at least once. The only question is when — and that's where races are won and lost.
Wait — what's a C3? Why does the commentator keep saying that?

Pirelli (F1's official tyre supplier) makes five tyre compounds in total, labelled C1 through C5. C1 is the toughest and slowest. C5 is the softest and fastest. Every race weekend, Pirelli selects three of those five to bring — then simply relabels them as Soft, Medium, and Hard for that race.

The naming trick that trips everyone up: at Monaco, Pirelli brings C3, C4, and C5. They call the C3 the "Hard," the C4 the "Medium," and the C5 the "Soft." Next race at Monza, they bring C1, C2, C3 — and now the C3 is suddenly the "Soft." Same tyre, different name. So "he's on the C5 soft at Monaco" means exactly what it says — compound 5, labelled soft this weekend.

🌡️ THE TEMPERATURE THING
Pirelli doesn't just pick softer compounds at slow circuits because they're faster. They pick them because slow tracks don't generate enough heat in the tyre. A C1 hard at Monaco would never reach its working temperature — the driver would be sliding everywhere like it's an ice rink. Each compound has a temperature "window" where it actually grips. Get it wrong and you're swimming.
A pitstop takes 2 seconds. So why does it cost 22?

The actual tyre swap is insanely fast. But slowing into the pit lane, driving it at 80km/h, and getting back up to speed? That's where all the time goes.

~8s
Braking & driving into the pit box
~2.5s
Actual tyre change (21 mechanics, simultaneously)
~11s
Pit lane speed limit & rejoining the track
~22 seconds total
That's how far behind you fall every time you pit
🧠
So why bother pitting? Because fresh tyres are 1–2 seconds faster per lap. Over 30 laps that's 30–60 seconds gained back. The 22s is a loan — you pay it back in pace. But only if you time it right.
⚡ WORLD RECORD
The fastest ever pitstop is 1.80 seconds — Red Bull at the 2023 São Paulo GP. That's 21 mechanics, four wheel-guns, two jacks, all hitting their mark in perfect sync. One person 0.3s late and the whole team loses two positions.
The undercut — F1's sneakiest move

Stuck behind someone and can't overtake on track? The undercut lets you pass them through the pits instead. No wheel-to-wheel needed.

1
You're P2, stuck 2.5s behind P1
Ten laps, same gap. Monaco — impossible to overtake. Both cars on old C4 softs, getting slower every lap.
2
"Box box box." You pit one lap early.
Your rival stays out. You emerge from the pits ~20 seconds behind — but on brand new C4 softs. They're still on 29-lap-old rubber.
3
You're 1.5s per lap faster
Your tyres are flying. Their old tyres are dying. Every single lap you're chipping away at that 20-second gap.
4
They finally pit — you're already ahead
When they come in, you've recovered enough time to emerge in front. You passed them through the pit lane. No overtake required. 🎉

The overcut is the opposite: you stay out longer than your rival, let them pit first, then use your track position advantage to hold them off when you eventually stop. Works best when the pit exit is slow or there's traffic.
How many stops should you do?

The biggest call of any race. One stop costs less time overall but leaves you on dead rubber at the end. Two stops keeps pace up but costs 44 seconds total. Both can win.

🚨 THE SAFETY CAR WILDCARD
A crash brings out the Safety Car and slows everyone to ~80km/h. Pitting under a Safety Car costs only ~5 seconds instead of 22 — because the whole field is crawling. Teams that react in under 10 seconds can jump 5 positions. Teams that hesitate lose the race. It's why strategy rooms go completely silent the moment a car stops on track.
Quick quiz 🏁

Three questions. No pressure — you're not being timed. (Unlike the pitstop crews.)

Q1. Monaco uses C3, C4, C5 tyres. Monza uses C1, C2, C3. Why the difference?
A Pirelli rotates compounds randomly each race
B Monza's high-speed straights generate far more heat and wear — so harder compounds are needed
C Monaco's longer track needs softer tyres to last the distance
✅ Exactly. Monza's long flat-out sections generate extreme tyre heat and load. Softer compounds would overheat and blister. Monaco's tight streets barely stress tyres at all — so they need the softest compounds just to get them up to working temperature.
❌ It's about heat and stress. Monza's speed hammers tyres — harder compounds (C1–C3) are needed to survive. Monaco barely stresses tyres so Pirelli brings the softest ones (C3–C5) just to get them to working temperature.
Q2. Leclerc pits on Lap 28 and comes out 20 seconds behind Verstappen. Is this a disaster?
A Yes — he's lost 20 seconds, the race is over for him
B No — if fresh tyres give him 1.5s per lap, he can recover that gap in about 14 laps
C Only a disaster if it rains
✅ Right! 1.5s × 14 laps = 21 seconds recovered. Verstappen's tyres are getting slower while Leclerc's new ones are flying. The 20s is just a loan — paid back in pace over the next stint.
❌ Think about the pace gap. Fresh tyres give ~1.5s per lap advantage. 20s ÷ 1.5s = only 14 laps to claw it back. With 50 laps left, that's very doable. The 20s isn't a loss — it's a loan paid back in pace.
Q3. A Safety Car comes out on Lap 40 with 38 laps to go. Hamilton is P3 on 25-lap-old mediums. What should Mercedes do?
A Stay out — track position is everything in F1
B Pit immediately — under Safety Car it costs only ~5s instead of 22s
C Wait and see what the cars ahead do
✅ Pit immediately. Under the Safety Car, pitting costs ~5s not 22s. Hamilton gets fresh rubber for basically nothing. On 38 laps of old mediums he'd be a sitting duck. The teams that hesitate here are the ones watching the podium from the pit wall.
❌ The Safety Car window is too good to ignore. It turns a 22-second penalty into a 5-second one. Hamilton on 25-lap-old mediums with 38 laps left will get overtaken by everyone on fresher rubber. Reacting in 10 seconds is the difference between winning and 5th place.
