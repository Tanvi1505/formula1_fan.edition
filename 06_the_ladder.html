F1 is the top of a ladder. Every driver you watch on Sunday clawed their way up through the series below. Here's a quick look at each one — and what it takes to reach F1.

Formula 2 — one step below F1

F2 is the final proving ground before Formula 1. The same circuits, the same race weekends, but a spec car — meaning every team uses an identical chassis and engine. Only driving talent separates them.

12
Teams, 2 drivers each = 24 drivers
Spec
Every car is identical — pure driver skill
3
Races per weekend — feature, sprint, reverse-grid
FIA
Mandatory for F1 Super Licence eligibility
The Super Licence: to drive in F1 you need an FIA Super Licence. To get one, you must accumulate 40 points by finishing well in junior championships. Winning F2 alone gives you the full 40. This is why F2 teams are essentially F1 talent agencies.

WHY IT'S ON THE SAME WEEKEND
F2 races happen on the same weekend as F1 — Friday practice, Saturday sprint, Sunday feature. This gives F2 drivers experience of the exact same circuit layout, atmosphere and pressure as F1. It also means F1 team bosses can watch their junior drivers from the pit wall, a few garages down.
What makes it hard: spec cars mean you can't blame the machinery. If you're slow in F2, it's you. The psychological pressure is immense — some highly-rated drivers never make it out of F2. Others win the title and still don't get an F1 seat because there are no vacancies.
Formula 3 — learning at the sharp end

F3 is two steps below F1. Also on the same race weekend as F1 and F2. Younger drivers, less powerful cars, but the same high-pressure environment. The step where raw karting talent first meets proper single-seater racing.

30
Drivers — the most competitive field per race
~380
Horsepower — roughly a third of an F1 car
17+
Typical driver age — some as young as 16
Spec
Identical Dallara cars — no team advantage
The reverse-grid chaos: F3 uses a reversed top-10 grid for the Saturday sprint race. The fastest qualifier starts 10th. This creates frantic racing — the fast drivers carving through traffic — and gives teams data on racecraft under pressure. Qualifying last in F3 is almost an advantage for Sunday's race.

THE FAMOUS MACAU GRAND PRIX
The Macau Grand Prix — a street circuit in the Chinese territory of Macau — is the most prestigious non-championship F3 race. Winning at Macau almost guarantees attention from F1 teams. Past winners include Senna, Schumacher, Verstappen, and Leclerc. It's F3's equivalent of a World Championship.
Formula 4 — the first real single-seater

F4 is where most F1 drivers' careers actually begin. After years of karting, a driver steps into their first proper single-seater car with exposed wheels. F4 is national or regional — there are separate championships in Britain, Germany, Italy, UAE, and more.

160
Horsepower — much less power, focus on fundamentals
15–17
Typical driver age — you can compete from 15
€300k
Approximate annual budget — expensive, but F1's cheapest rung
Safe
Halo, roll hoop, strict crash tests — built for young drivers
The reality of F4: most drivers here are paying to race — backed by family money, sponsors, or junior programs from F1 teams. An F1 team's junior academy (like Ferrari's or Red Bull's) will fund a promising driver's F4 season in exchange for first right of negotiation on their future career.

THE COST OF THE DREAM
Getting from karts to F1 can cost €5–10 million in total — most of it spent before the driver ever earns a penny from racing. This is why very few F1 drivers come from ordinary families, and why "pay drivers" — those who bring sponsorship money to teams — are a controversial but economically real part of the sport.
Formula E — F1's fully electric cousin

Formula E is not a stepping stone to F1. It's a separate world championship for fully electric single-seater racing cars. Same prestige, different philosophy — city circuits, sustainable technology, and a very different kind of race.

Attack Mode: Formula E's version of an overtaking boost. Drivers must deliberately drive off the racing line through a designated zone to activate a power boost. This means you lose time and track position to gain power — a tactical decision that makes races unpredictable and exciting.

THE MANUFACTURER APPEAL
Formula E attracts car manufacturers differently from F1. Porsche, Jaguar, Nissan, DS, Maserati — brands that don't race in F1 compete in Formula E, because the technology is directly relevant to their electric road cars. For them, it's the most valuable motorsport laboratory in the world.
The sound: Formula E cars are nearly silent compared to F1. Some fans miss the engine noise. Others find the eerie electric whine surprisingly tense. Racing through city streets at 280km/h, past apartment buildings and restaurants, with only a high-pitched electric hum — it's a completely different atmosphere.
