Saturday decides Sunday. Here's the knockout system that sets the starting grid — and why the last few minutes of Q3 are some of the most tense moments in motorsport.

Why does Saturday matter?

The starting grid for Sunday's race is not random. It's earned on Saturday in a session called qualifying — or quali. Where you start has a massive impact on where you finish. Starting at the front means clean air, no traffic, and the ability to control your own race.

The key idea: qualifying is a knockout. Start with 20 drivers. Cut to 15. Cut to 10. The final 10 fight for pole. The slowest drivers in each round go to the back of the grid.
Q1 — eighteen minutes, five eliminated

All 20 drivers are on track. Everyone must set a lap time. The 5 slowest drivers at the end of Q1 are eliminated — they start the race from positions 16 to 20. The other 15 progress to Q2.

Q1 — 18 MINUTES, ALL 20 DRIVERS
PROGRESSES TO Q2
ELIMINATED — START P16 TO P20
The Q1 pressure: this is where the midfield teams are really racing. Williams vs Haas vs Alpine — fighting for the final few spots that progress to Q2. For the top teams, Q1 is a formality. For everyone else, it's survival.
STRATEGY NOTE
Teams often send drivers out on medium tyres in Q1 to save their soft tyres for Q3. If a driver is confident they'll progress easily, there's no point burning a new set of softs just to qualify 12th instead of 11th.
Q2 — fifteen minutes, five more eliminated

The 15 remaining drivers compete. Again, the 5 slowest are eliminated — they start the race in positions 11 to 15. The fastest 10 go through to Q3.

The Q2 tyre rule: whatever tyre compound a driver uses to set their Q2 time, they must start the race on that compound. This creates a fascinating strategic choice — do you go for it on soft tyres (faster lap, but you start the race on a tyre that wears quickly), or do you set your Q2 time on mediums and start the race on a more durable compound?
WHY THIS MATTERS
A driver who qualifies 9th on medium tyres may actually have an advantage at the race start over a driver who qualifies 8th on softs. The medium-tyre driver can run longer before pitting. This rule only applies to drivers who progress — the Q2 eliminated drivers can start on any tyre they like.
Q3 — twelve minutes, ten drivers, one pole

This is what everyone watches for. The top 10 drivers, fresh sets of soft tyres, track conditions improving every lap. Each driver typically gets two flying laps — the fastest lap wins pole position.

TYPICAL Q3 TIMELINE — 12 MINUTES
0–4 MIN
First runs
Everyone does an out lap, then a flying lap. Establish a benchmark time.
4–8 MIN
Back to pits
Teams analyse data. Drivers get fresh soft tyres. The track is getting faster every lap — the rubber laid down increases grip.
8–12 MIN
The final lap
Everyone leaves the pits to time their flying lap for the final 2 minutes. Getting stuck in traffic here is catastrophic.
The traffic problem: every driver wants to do their best flying lap on the last possible moment — when the track is fastest and the rubber is most built up. But 10 drivers all trying to do this at the same time creates absolute chaos. Getting stuck behind a slow out lap car can cost half a second — and your pole position.
THE RED FLAG GAMBLE
If a driver crashes in the final minute of Q3, a red flag stops the session. Any driver who hadn't completed a flying lap yet gets another attempt — an unexpected second chance. Teams sometimes gamble by sending their car out at the last possible moment to maximise track evolution, knowing a red flag could destroy their session.
You qualified 3rd. You start 8th. How?

Grid penalties are applied after qualifying for a range of reasons. A driver who qualifies brilliantly can find themselves starting much further back because of something that happened before the session even started.

Engine penalty
F1 limits the number of engine components per season. Use more than the allocation and you get a grid penalty — typically 5, 10, or 15 places. Teams often take these penalties at circuits where starting position matters less.
MOST COMMON
Impeding in qualifying
If a driver blocks another driver during their flying lap in qualifying and it's deemed to have cost them time, they receive a grid penalty. Getting in someone's way in Q3 can ruin two sessions at once.
AVOIDABLE
Causing a collision
If a driver is judged to have caused a collision in the previous race, a grid penalty can be applied to the next race. The stewards review incidents after the race and can hand retrospective penalties.
RETROSPECTIVE
Pit lane start
For major technical infringements, or if too many penalties accumulate, a driver starts from the pit lane — behind every car on the grid. A disaster. The team can at least make setup changes to the car while waiting.
WORST CASE
Teams strategically choose when to take engine penalties. If a driver needs a new engine component and faces a 10-place penalty, they'll try to take it at a circuit where overtaking is easy (like Spa or Monza) rather than Monaco, where starting position is almost everything.
Quick quiz

Three questions on qualifying. You've got this.

Q1. In Q2, a driver sets their fastest time on soft tyres. What does this mean for the race?
A Nothing — they can start on any tyre they want
B They must start the race on soft tyres — which wear out faster
C They get a 5-place grid penalty for using soft tyres in Q2
Correct. The Q2 tyre rule forces drivers to start the race on whatever compound they used to set their Q2 time. Setting it on softs is faster but means starting the race on a tyre that wears out sooner.
Not quite. The Q2 tyre rule: the compound a driver uses to set their Q2 time is the compound they must start the race on. Using softs in Q2 locks you into starting the race on softs — which burn out faster.
Q2. Why do drivers try to do their final Q3 lap as late as possible?
A To intimidate their rivals by going last
B The track gets faster over time as rubber builds up from all the laps driven
C Tyre temperature drops on the out lap if you go too early
Exactly right. Each lap lays down rubber on the racing line. The track surface becomes grippier as the session goes on. The very last lap of Q3 is usually the fastest lap possible — which is why everyone fights to do it at the last moment.
The main reason is track evolution. Each lap lays rubber on the track, making it grippier and grippier. The last flying lap of Q3 is usually done on the fastest track surface of the whole weekend — which is why everyone wants to go last.
Q3. A driver qualifies 2nd but takes a 10-place grid penalty. Where do they start the race?
A 12th — they drop 10 places from 2nd
B Last — penalties always send you to the back
C They don't start — penalties mean disqualification
Right. 2nd place minus 10 = 12th. Simple arithmetic. If enough other drivers also had penalties, the actual starting position could be slightly different — but the base rule is your qualifying position minus the penalty places.
Grid penalties are subtracted from your qualifying position. 2nd minus 10 = 12th. Unless they accumulate so many penalties they literally run out of grid positions — then they'd start from the pit lane.

Watch for this on Saturday: in the final 90 seconds of Q3, count how many cars are on out laps and how many are on flying laps. The ones on flying laps are going for it. The ones on out laps are warming their tyres and will cross the line just as the clock hits zero. That's the moment that decides Sunday.
