How does the
championship
work?

Points, titles, two championships running at once. Here's the full scoring system — and why a team can win the title even when their driver doesn't.

There are actually two championships.

Every race weekend, points are being scored in two completely separate competitions running simultaneously — one for individual drivers, one for teams.

WORLD DRIVERS' CHAMPIONSHIP
Points earned by each individual driver
The driver with the most points at season end wins the WDC
The most famous title in motorsport
Lando Norris won it in 2025
CONSTRUCTORS' CHAMPIONSHIP
Points from both team drivers combined
The team with the most combined points wins the WCC
Worth hundreds of millions in prize money
McLaren won it in 2025
A team can win the Constructors' title even if neither of their drivers wins the Drivers' title. If both Ferrari drivers consistently finish P2 and P3 while one Red Bull driver wins but the other crashes, Ferrari could accumulate more team points despite not having the individual champion.
How many points does each position earn?

Points are awarded to the top 10 finishers in every race. There is also one bonus point available — for the driver who sets the fastest lap, but only if they finish in the top 10.

POINTS PER FINISHING POSITION
THE FASTEST LAP POINT
One bonus point is awarded for the fastest lap of the race — but only if the driver finishing in the top 10 sets it. Teams sometimes pit a driver in the closing laps specifically to put them on fresh soft tyres and chase the fastest lap point. It sounds small, but in a tight championship, 1 point can be the difference.
A win is worth 25 points. That means if you win every single race, you'd score 25 × 24 = 600 points in a 24-race season (before fastest lap bonuses). In reality, no driver has ever done that — reliability, crashes and competition always intervene.
Sprint races — bonus points on Saturdays

At 6 race weekends per season, there's a sprint race on Saturday — a shorter race of roughly 100km (about 30% of a full Grand Prix). Points are on offer, no mandatory pit stop.

Sprint weekends have a different structure: Sprint Qualifying on Friday afternoon sets the sprint grid. The sprint race runs Saturday morning. Then normal qualifying sets the Sunday race grid. Two qualifying sessions, two races in one weekend.
How is the championship actually decided?

It's cumulative. Every race, every sprint race, every fastest lap — points accumulate across the entire season. The driver (and team) with the most points after the final race wins.

Consistency beats occasional brilliance. A driver who finishes P3 every race will beat a driver who wins 6 races but retires from 8. The championship rewards reliability as much as speed. This is why protecting your car in a battle — accepting a slower position rather than risking a crash — is often the smarter call.
EXAMPLE: 5-RACE TITLE FIGHT
DRIVER R1 R2 R3 R4 R5 TOTAL
Driver A 25 0 DNF 25 0 DNF 25 75
Driver B 15 18 18 15 18 84
Driver A won 3 races. Driver B won zero. Driver B wins the championship — by 9 points. Consistency wins.
Quick quiz

Three questions on points and championships.

Q1. Ferrari's two drivers finish P1 and P4. McLaren's two drivers finish P2 and P3. Who scores more Constructors' points?
A Ferrari — they won the race, which counts more
B McLaren — P2+P3 gives 18+15=33 pts, vs Ferrari's P1+P4 of 25+12=37 pts... actually Ferrari
C Ferrari — 25+12=37 points beats McLaren's 18+15=33
Yes — Ferrari scores 37 combined (25+12) vs McLaren's 33 (18+15). A race win has huge value in both championships. Even with two drivers on the podium, McLaren falls behind because P1 scores so much more than P2.
Ferrari: P1 = 25, P4 = 12, total = 37. McLaren: P2 = 18, P3 = 15, total = 33. Ferrari leads by 4 points despite McLaren having two podium finishes. The race win carries enormous weight.
Q2. How many points does a sprint race winner receive?
A 25 — same as a full Grand Prix win
B 8 — sprint races give out much smaller points
C 15 — roughly half a normal race win
Correct — 8 points for a sprint race win. Points go down to P8 (1 point). Sprint races are shorter and less prestigious, so the points are much smaller — but they still count toward both championships.
Sprint race winner gets 8 points — much less than a full Grand Prix win (25). Points go to P8 (1 point minimum). Small numbers, but they can still swing a tight championship fight.
Q3. Driver A has 340 points with 2 races left. Driver B has 320 points. Can Driver B still win the title?
A No — Driver A needs to lose 20 points which isn't possible
B Yes — 2 wins + 2 fastest laps = 52 pts max available. B needs 21 more than A, so if B wins both and A scores nothing, B wins
C No — once you're behind with 2 races left it's mathematically impossible
Correct. Driver B can score up to 52 points (2×26 including fastest lap). If B scores 52 and A scores 0, B ends on 372 vs A's 340. It's unlikely but mathematically alive. This is why teams talk about "keeping the championship alive."
It's still mathematically possible. 2 races × 26 points max (25+1 fastest lap) = 52 available. B is 20 behind. If B takes maximum points and A scores very little, B can overturn it. This exact scenario plays out nearly every season.
