What does
every flag
mean?

Marshals wave them. Drivers obey them. Ignoring them costs you — penalties, disqualification, or worse. Here's your complete guide.

Why flags at all? Don't they have radios?

Drivers do have radios — but at 300km/h, a driver might miss a radio call or not have time to act on it. Flags are a universal, instant, always-visible signal. They're used by marshals stationed around the entire circuit and must be obeyed immediately.

In modern F1, most flag signals are also shown on display panels around the track and on the steering wheel itself. But the physical flag tradition remains — partly for redundancy, partly because it's been part of motor racing since the very beginning.

Safety flags — these are serious

When you see these, something significant has happened on track. Every driver knows exactly what each one means and what they must do immediately.

WAVED OR STATIONARY
Yellow Flag
Danger ahead. Slow down, no overtaking. A waved yellow (moving) means more danger than a stationary one. Drivers must demonstrably reduce speed in the yellow sector. Ignoring one is a serious offence — typically a 10-second penalty or worse.
SLOW DOWN · NO OVERTAKING
SHOWN AT START LINE
Red Flag
Session stopped. All cars must slow down immediately and return to the pit lane — no overtaking. Red flags happen when there's a serious crash, debris covering the track, or dangerous weather conditions. Races can be restarted after a red flag, sometimes on a rolling start.
STOP IMMEDIATELY · SESSION HALTED
SHOWN AT START LINE
Red and Yellow Striped Flag
Slippery surface ahead. Oil, water, or debris on the track surface. Take extra caution — the car may lose grip unexpectedly. Often seen after rain starts, when oil is dropped, or when a car sheds a part.
GRIP HAZARD AHEAD
SHOWN AT MARSHAL POST
White Flag
Slow-moving vehicle ahead — usually a recovery vehicle, medical car, or safety car in the sector. Be prepared to encounter something moving slowly around the next corner. Less common in modern F1 but still used.
SLOW VEHICLE AHEAD
Flags aimed at specific drivers

Some flags are directed at individual cars — either telling a driver they're doing something wrong, or warning them about what's coming behind them.

SHOWN TO A SPECIFIC CAR WITH NUMBER BOARD
Black Flag
You are disqualified. The most severe flag in F1. The driver must return to the pits immediately and is excluded from the race. Reasons include ignoring a drive-through penalty, serious technical infringement, or extremely dangerous driving. Very rare — almost never seen in modern F1.
DISQUALIFIED · RETURN TO PITS
SHOWN WITH NUMBER BOARD
Black and White Flag
Official warning for unsportsmanlike behaviour. A formal warning before a black flag. Typically shown for blocking, dangerous driving, or repeated track limit violations. One black and white flag per driver per race maximum — a second offence goes straight to a penalty.
OFFICIAL WARNING
SHOWN WITH NUMBER BOARD
Black and Orange Flag (Meatball Flag)
Technical problem with your car — return to the pits. Shown when a driver has a mechanical issue that could be dangerous to themselves or others. The driver must pit for repairs or retirement. Named "meatball" because of its appearance — the orange circle on black.
PIT IMMEDIATELY — MECHANICAL ISSUE
SHOWN REPEATEDLY TO A SPECIFIC DRIVER
Blue Flag
A faster car is about to lap you — move over. Shown to a driver being caught by a car that is about to lap them (one full lap ahead). The driver being lapped must allow the faster car through within 3 blue flags or they receive a penalty. Backmarkers being lapped must not impede the race leaders.
LET THE LEADER THROUGH
The two everyone knows — and one they don't
FINISH LINE · END OF SESSION
Chequered Flag
The race is over. Waved at the finish line as the leader crosses. Every driver must complete their current lap and return to the pits. The positions at the moment each driver crosses the line after the chequered flag determine the final race result.
RACE FINISHED
SHOWN AT THE START OF SESSION
Green Flag
Track is clear, racing resumes. Shown after a yellow flag period ends, or at the start of a session. When the Safety Car returns to the pits, drivers see green flags indicating racing can resume. The driver behind the Safety Car can choose when to accelerate — which is itself a tactical decision.
CLEAR TRACK · RACING RESUMES
The Safety Car restart moment is one of the most exciting in F1. The leader controls the pace behind the Safety Car — they can slow the pack right down then suddenly accelerate, trying to catch the cars behind them unprepared. Or surge early to build a gap before the Safety Car line.
Quick quiz

Three flag questions. They come up more often than you'd think during a race.

Q1. A marshal waves a yellow flag at a driver. What must the driver do?
A Stop the car immediately and wait
B Slow down and not overtake — there's danger in that sector
C Return to the pits at the end of the lap
Correct. Yellow flag = slow down, no overtaking. There's an incident or hazard ahead. Ignoring one typically earns a minimum 10-second penalty. Drivers must show a measurable reduction in speed through the yellow sector.
Yellow flag means slow down and no overtaking — there's danger ahead. Not a full stop (that's a red flag). Not a pit instruction. Just: reduce speed and don't pass anyone in that sector.
Q2. A blue flag is shown to Carlos Sainz. What does this mean?
A Sainz must pit on the next lap
B Sainz is about to be lapped — he must let the faster car through
C There's a wet patch on track ahead
Right. Blue flag means a car that's a full lap ahead is approaching and Sainz must let them through without a fight. He has 3 blue flags to comply. Failing to do so = a penalty. Backmarkers sometimes hold up leaders by mistake or deliberately, which is why this rule exists.
Blue flag = a lapping car is approaching. Sainz must move aside and let the faster car through. He gets 3 blue flags before a penalty is applied. This keeps backmarkers from interfering with the race leaders' fight.
Q3. The red flag comes out mid-race. What happens next?
A The race result is decided at that moment — whoever leads wins
B All cars return to the pits. The race may restart after the problem is resolved
C A Safety Car is deployed and racing continues slowly
Correct. Red flag stops the session entirely — all cars return to the pits. Race control then decides whether to restart. If they do, the grid is usually reset to the order from a few laps before the red flag. The restart can be a standing start or rolling start. Strategy chaos often follows.
Red flag = full stop. All cars pit. Race control assesses the situation. If conditions allow, the race restarts — either from a standing or rolling start. The result from the moment of the red flag may count if the race cannot be restarted.

Watch for this next race: when the Safety Car comes out, look for the green flag or green light panels around the circuit signalling the restart. And watch the leader — they'll decide exactly when to accelerate, and everyone behind must react instantly. That gap in the first corner after restart often decides the race.
